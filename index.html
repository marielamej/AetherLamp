Mariela Mejia-Guzman's Process

Technology & Programming Lead

Initial Testing

Early Circuit Exploration

I began with a simple light-sensitive circuit using a photoresistor, resistor, an LED strip, and an Arduino Uno. The goal was to have the LEDs respond to ambient light‚Äîbrightening in the dark and dimming in the light. While the initial test worked, I noticed inconsistencies in how the brightness reacted. This raised questions about whether the issue was the sensor, the circuit, or my code. It was a valuable starting point for understanding the sensitivity of light inputs and how they translate to visual output.

Lighting Behavior

Circadian Light

Inspired by tools like f.lux, I explored how to mimic natural light transitions throughout the day. This led me to research microcontrollers with WiFi capability. I chose the ESP8266 and learned how to connect it to the internet to fetch the current time via an NTP server and pull sunrise/sunset data from an API. This data would eventually control the brightness and color temperature of the LEDs, creating a light flow that adjusts according to real-world sunlight patterns.

Technical Challenges

Troubleshooting & Technical Growth

Hardware setup wasn‚Äôt without complications. I quickly realized my original power source couldn‚Äôt provide enough current for the LED strip. I spent time learning about regulated 5V/2A adapters and proper power distribution. Additionally, the ESP8266 wasn‚Äôt recognized by my computer at first. This setback taught me about USB data cables vs. power-only cables, driver installation (CH340), and board manager settings in the Arduino IDE. These frustrations ultimately deepened my understanding of hardware reliability.

Prototype Development

Base & Physical Prototype Development

While I was still solving circuit issues, I also turned attention to the lamp's physical structure. I reused a pole and ceramic lampshade from a previous project and designed a custom base in SolidWorks. The base included an internal slot for the microcontroller and a routed exit for the power cable. I shortened the pole and ran wiring cleanly through its interior, keeping the aesthetic minimal. This stage helped me visualize how all components would physically integrate and guided the layout for future iterations.

Final Development

Final Troubleshooting

To simplify the setup and ensure stability, I stripped a USB power cable and connected it directly to the circuit. I then added an inline switch that allows the lamp to be easily powered on or off. This eliminated the need to plug and unplug wires during testing and made the prototype more practical for everyday use. I also tested how to power both the microcontroller and LED strip from a single source, ensuring the circuit wouldn't overload.

Final Design

Redesign & Physical Assembly

For the final prototype, I transitioned away from the early mock-up and designed a completely new circular base from scratch. The new base was 3D modeled and fabricated to securely house the electronics, with a built-in notch for clean cable management. I also created a new, sturdier pole to better support the lamp shade and give the piece a more balanced, finished appearance. This version not only improved the structural integrity of the lamp but also elevated its aesthetic to better reflect the intended calming, functional design for everyday use.

Kaya Hamon's Process

Design Lead

Planning Phase

Initial Setup

I began with a planning meeting with my mentor, John Domenico, where we established the project scope and vision. I created a semester timeline with key milestones, determined what materials I‚Äôd need, and developed a strategy for slip casting using push pins.

Form Development

Sketching & Prototyping

I sketched multiple lamp designs, focusing on aesthetics and light diffusion. After selecting three strong concepts, I built physical prototypes and documented the process. I then gathered feedback from five students to evaluate form, function, and visual appeal.

Refinement Phase

Iterating on Feedback

Based on user feedback, I refined the preferred form into three different sizes. I recorded the process and conducted another round of user testing. After reviewing feedback with my mentor, I adjusted my casting strategy and prepared a new clay formulation.

Production Development

Mold & Glaze Testing

I developed a multi-part mold over four days using push pins for precision. Then, I tested and formulated glazes over two days, creating test tiles to evaluate light diffusion and aesthetics. I documented all technical steps and glaze outcomes carefully.

Casting Phase

Porcelain Slip Casting

My first casting attempts included a few failures, which I documented and learned from. I adjusted slip consistency and successfully completed two porcelain casts. These were bisque fired on March 24 and glaze fired on April 2, with temperature curves tracked.

Final Development

Presentation & Documentation

I compiled and edited my process videos and visual assets into a professional presentation. I conducted multiple practice sessions to prepare and ensured the narrative followed a clear, structured format, showcasing each step of my development process.
